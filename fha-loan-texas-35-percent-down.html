On a recent episode of Mortgage Talk LIVE, a first-time buyer in Houston asked a question I hear almost every week: "Can I really buy a house with just 3.5% down — or is that too good to be true?"

The short answer: yes, it's completely real. And in this article, I'm going to walk you through exactly how it works — the credit requirements, the actual costs involved, down payment assistance options in Texas, and the step-by-step process from application to keys in hand.

💡 Kenny's Take

FHA loans are one of the most powerful tools for first-time buyers. But they're not always the cheapest option once you factor in mortgage insurance. Read through before you decide — I want you to make the right call for your situation.

What Is an FHA Loan?

FHA stands for Federal Housing Administration. The FHA doesn't lend money directly — instead, it insures loans made by approved lenders. That insurance is what allows lenders to offer more flexible terms than conventional loans.

Because the government backs the loan, lenders are willing to accept borrowers with lower credit scores and smaller down payments than they'd normally allow. The trade-off is that you pay for that insurance in the form of a mortgage insurance premium (MIP).

FHA Loan Requirements for Texas Buyers

Here's what you'll need to qualify for an FHA loan in 2026:

Requirement Minimum Notes
Credit Score 580 For the 3.5% down option
Credit Score (with 10% down) 500–579 Higher down payment required
Down Payment 3.5% Of the purchase price
Debt-to-Income Ratio 43–50% max Some lenders allow up to 57% with compensating factors
Employment History 2 years Same field or stable employment
Property Type Primary residence Can't use FHA for investment properties

The 580 Credit Score Rule

One thing that trips people up: the "580 minimum" is the FHA's guideline, but individual lenders often overlay their own requirements. Some lenders won't go below 620 even on an FHA loan. ⚠️ Don't Forget Closing Costs

Down payment is just one piece. Closing costs in Texas typically run 2–3% of the loan amount. On a $375K purchase, that's another $7,500–$11,000. The good news: closing costs can often be negotiated as a seller concession, or covered by down payment assistance programs — which I'll cover next.

Down Payment Assistance Programs in Texas

This is where it gets interesting. Texas has some of the most generous down payment assistance programs in the country. Many buyers go directly to their bank and never hear about these — because banks can't always offer them. As a broker, I can.

My First Texas Home (MFTH)

Offered through the Texas State Affordable Housing Corporation (TSAHC). Provides up to 5% of the loan amount as a grant or forgivable second lien. Available to first-time buyers and veterans.

Texas Mortgage Credit Certificate (MCC)

This one is often overlooked. An MCC converts a portion of your mortgage interest into a dollar-for-dollar federal tax credit — up to $2,000 per year. It doesn't reduce your rate, but it reduces your tax bill every year you live in the home.

SE Texas Housing Finance Corporation

Covers the Houston metro and surrounding counties. Offers down payment grants up to 4% for qualifying buyers. No repayment required if you stay in the home.

DPA programs have income limits and purchase price caps. Many buyers assume they don't qualify — but I've run the numbers for hundreds of buyers who were surprised to find they did. Before you assume you're out, let me check your file. It takes five minutes.

FHA Mortgage Insurance: The Real Cost

Here's the part most articles skip over. FHA loans require two types of mortgage insurance:

  • Upfront MIP: 1.75% of the loan amount — typically rolled into the loan
  • Annual MIP: 0.55% per year on most loans, paid monthly

On a $361,875 loan (from our $375K example above), that's:

MIP Type Rate Monthly Cost
Upfront MIP (rolled in) 1.75% Adds ~$6,333 to loan
Annual MIP 0.55% ~$166/month

The annual MIP stays on for the life of the loan if you put less than 10% down. This is one of the main reasons why — once you've built equity — refinancing into a conventional loan can save you a significant amount of money.

Step-by-Step: How to Buy in Texas with FHA

  • 1
    Check Your Credit Score

    Pull your credit from all three bureaus. You need 580+ for 3.5% down. If you're below, I can show you the fastest legal ways to boost your score — sometimes in as little as 30–60 days.

  • 2
    Get Pre-Approved (Not Pre-Qualified)

    Pre-approval involves a full credit review and income verification. Pre-qualification is just a ballpark. In Texas's competitive market, you need a real pre-approval letter before making an offer.

  • 3
    Identify Your DPA Options

    Before you start shopping for homes, we determine which down payment assistance programs you qualify for. This shapes what price range makes sense for you.

  • 4
    Find a Home and Make an Offer

    Work with a buyer's agent (free to you). The home must meet FHA property standards — major structural issues can disqualify a property, so your agent should know what to look for.

  • 5
    FHA Appraisal and Underwriting

    FHA appraisals are stricter than conventional — they check both value and condition. Underwriting reviews all your documents. On average, this takes 2–3 weeks.

  • 6
    Clear to Close → Closing Day

    Once underwriting approves your loan, you get a Clear to Close. The average time to close with my team is 14 days from CTC. You'll sign final documents and get your keys.

Is FHA Always the Best Option?

Not always. Here's when to consider alternatives:

  • 620+ credit score: A conventional loan with 3–5% down might have lower total cost once you factor in no lifetime MIP obligation
  • Military veteran: VA loan at 0% down with no mortgage insurance is almost always better than FHA
  • Rural area: USDA loan at 0% down might be available — often overlooked
  • Below 580 credit: FHA at 10% down or work on credit first — conventional isn't accessible yet
🎯 Bottom Line

FHA is a great tool — especially when paired with down payment assistance. But the right loan depends on your full picture: credit, income, savings, how long you plan to stay, and whether you qualify for VA or USDA. That's why you talk to a broker, not just a bank.
